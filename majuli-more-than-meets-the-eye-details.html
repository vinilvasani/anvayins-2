Majuli - More Than Meets the Eye

If there is one place in northeastern India that has not received the recognition it deserves, it is Majuli. The world’s largest river island, it is one of the most important centres of Vaishnavism, a major Hindu tradition, and is famous for its satras or Vaishnavite monasteries. Unfortunately, some of Majuli’s other attractions have always remained in the shadows of its Vaishnavite glory.

Take birdwatching for example. An abundance of wetlands makes Majuli an ideal habit for a wide variety of resident as well as migratory birds including some uncommon species. Apparently, more than 250 species of birds have been recorded on the island. Some people have even called it a heaven for birdwatchers.

After an early morning spent in birdwatching, have a relaxed breakfast and then head out on a bicycle through the near empty Majuli roads along bright green paddy fields. Stop for a chat with shepherds grazing their cows in wide open grasslands.

Visit the Mising tribals who have made Majuli their home ever since they migrated here from Arunachal Pradesh around the 13th century. They are known to still follow a strongly animistic belief system. They are also known for weaving gadu, a type of blanket, and elegant Assamese tapestries, both of which require a reasonable degree of skill and know-how which has passed down through generations. They are an extremely hospitable people and will be happy to have you spend a few hours with them, sharing their food, talking to them and watching them as they go about their weaving or pottery making.

Majuli is also home to the Deori tribals. The term Deori is said to be made of the three parts deu, o and r meaning wise, male and female respectively. The tribe is therefore known as ‘the tribe of wise people’. Another interpretation of the name is ‘the tribe of the people who worship God’. True to these names, they are considered to be the scholars among all the tribals in the region and are very religious people. Every house has a dedicated space for prayer and worship.

The star of any visit to Majuli, however, is Jadav Molai Payeng. Deeply affected by the adverse impact of deforestation on the biodiversity of Majuli, Jadav Molai joined a government appointed team of tree planters and even after the government project was over, single-handedly continued to plant trees on the island. He did this for the next 20 years and in the end had created a forest all by himself, earning himself the epithet of the Forest Man of India. Felicitated by the Indian government and other international bodies, Molai continues to live and work in Majuli, waking up at 3 am everyday to take care of his beloved forest and plant yet more trees.

Last but certainly not the least, spend some time visiting the aforementioned Vaishnavite satras of Majuli. Srimanta Sankaradeva, the 16th century saint and spiritual reformer and a staunch believer of Vaishnavism, established Majuli as its centre. He commissioned many of these satras as a place for monks committed to spirituality to live a simple and disciplined life. Every satra has a prayer hall called namghar, which is the venue not only for spiritual activities but also for important discussion related to the community. Shamaguri and Aunati are the better known satras among travellers; the first for its uniquely crafted masks and wooden sculptures and the second for its museum that houses artefacts of the Ahom kingdom that ruled Assam for more than 600 years.

While the satras insist on strict adherence to their code of conduct, they also allow a high degree of freedom to its monks. They are allowed to enrol in schools and work outside the satra and can even give up their monkhood at any time in their life if they so chose. Despite this, leavers are few and the larger monasteries house up to 600 monks at any given time. Talk to a monk as he goes about his daily routine and know what life is like for them.

Srimanta Sanakaradeva also instituted various forms of art, dance and music. Enjoy the unique Kshatriya form of dance that is recognised as one of the eight classical dances in India or watch gayan-bayan, an interesting blend of song, dance and music.

It is an unfortunate fact that Majuli with its riches of ecological and cultural treasures, has been eroding rapidly over the past few decades and may get completely submerged by the Brahmaputra and its tributaries in the next 20-30 years. Pay a visit to this mesmerising river island before it is too late.
