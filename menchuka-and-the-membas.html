Menchuka and the Membas

The town of Menchuka lies deep inside north-central Arunachal Pradesh—8 hours away from the nearest Indian town while only about 40km away from the Chinese border. It has started making an appearance on tourist maps only in the last decade, primarily because of its remote location but its exquisite natural beauty coupled with old-world charm make a trip here worthwhile.

Heritage

The first glimpse of Menchuka gives the impression of a place frozen in time—an extensive flatland amidst mountains of brown and green, garlanded by snow clad peaks on three sides, and the azure Yargapchu river meandering its way over the vast plain dotted with colourful Tibetan houses. A few of these houses are built in the traditional style, without the use of nails and clamps.

Menchuka is inhabited primarily by the Memba tribals—Buddhists of Mongoloid stock and a particularly genial people. They have lived here for centuries, as a result of which they can boast of a rich cultural and mythological heritage. Menchuka itself is believed to have been blessed into existence by Guru Rinpoche, the 8 th century saint who is revered by many as the ‘second Buddha’. As evidence, the locals urge you to look around the valley and its surroundings which resemble the shape of a lotus—the flower that Guru Rinpoche is believed to have been born out of.

Festivals

This and other myths grant Menchuka holy status. The Buddhists in and around the region refer to the town as Neh Nang, meaning pilgrimage. Menchuka’s holy associations are believed to have endowed its snowmelt with medicinal properties, hence the name Menchuka where men is medicine, chu is water and kha is snow.

The air in this region is so unpolluted that it allows the growth of rare lichens on rocks and tree barks. It also makes a walk or cycle ride through the town especially invigorating. You can venture further out to the beautiful town of Dorjeeling that has a Gompa with a large statue of Buddha.. On the way back, a trek across the Menchuka La, the traditional route between the two towns, can help to work up an appetite for some delicious local food.

Homestays

Myths and spirituality pervade all aspects of life in Menchuka and as such their symbols abound in and around the place. Only 15 kms away, on the Indo-Tibetan road is Taposthan, meaning ‘place of penance’ or Pemo Shubu, as the locals call it. They believe that Guru Rinpoche once meditated here. Interestingly, the same spot is also considered sacred by a Sikh contingent of the Indian army stationed there, who believe that Guru Nanak, founder of the Sikh religion, had an encounter with a wild bear here and overpowered him with his spiritual powers. The army has built a Gurudwara to immortalise the story and operate a community kitchen which provides ‘langar’ or free food everyday at 2 pm. Differences notwithstanding, both the beliefs have coexisted in harmony for decades.

Another spot of spiritual significance for the Membas is Lhalung, meaning God’s (lha) place (lung) and is believed to be the cradle of human settlement in the region. Apparently, aeons ago there was a dense forest here and a revered Tibetan lama cleared the area by invoking a landslide through meditative powers. The staff he planted before he sat down to meditate transformed into a sandalwood tree and the rock he sat on took the shape of a seat and developed imprints of his folded feet. The locals call the place Neh Chandan Takp where neh is pilgrimage, chandan is sandalwood and takp is dream.

Experiences

Yet another holy place is the Samten Yongcha monastery, popularly known as the Old Gompa, and believed to be older than the more famous Tawang Monastery. While it is believed to be over 400 years old, the present structure was rebuilt about 100 years ago after the original building burnt down in a massive fire. Among the idols at the monastery, the local favourite is Tardin Chejlingb, tales of whose spiritual powers are told reverently. The locals believe that during the time of the great fire, the idol of Tardin Chejlingb made its way out of the gompa on its own and was thus unscathed. It is the one to look out for when visiting the monastery.
