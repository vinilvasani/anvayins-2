Nagaland

Aoleang Festival

Aoleang is the most important annual festival of the Konyak Naga tribe of Nagaland in northeastern India. The largest in the region, the Konyaks are primarily an agrarian tribe and practice slash and burn cultivation, also known as jhum in the local tongue. Aoleang is celebrated at the end of the sowing season to usher in spring with its blooming red coral trees and red lilies signaling the beginning of a new year. The Konyaks use the opportunity to pray to the divine spirits for a good harvest.

Hornbill Festival

The Hornbill festival held every year in the Indian state of Nagaland is a ten day long cultural extravaganza. Named after the colourful tropical bird that abounds in this region, the festival was conceived a couple of decades ago to provide an opportunity to the 16 Naga tribes to gather and showcase various aspects of their culture and tradition, thereby also ensuring their survival.

Arunachal Pradesh

Mopin Festival of the Galos

The Galo tribe of Arunachal Pradesh is a primarily agrarian society. Consequently its most important festival, the Mopin, falls during the sowing season. The tribespeople pray to Mopin Ane, the Goddess of prosperity, for a bounteous harvest and the banishment of evil spirits. Unlike other Arunachali festivals which originated as family affairs, Mopin has always been a community festival. Though it is celebrated in all major towns and villages across East and West Siang districts, the biggest celebration takes place at Aalo, headquarter of the West Siang district.

The Sonam Losar Festival

Sonam Losar is the most important festival of the Memba tribe of Arunachal Pradesh and marks the beginning of the Buddhist new year. With sonam meaning good fortune, lo meaning year and sar meaning new, it is an occasion for the Membas to pray for a bountiful new year. In Menchuka, it is usually celebrated in the third week of January. Interestingly, other parts of Arunachal Pradesh as well as Tibet and Nepal, celebrate it approximately 15 days later.
