Nagaland

The Konyaks

The state of Nagaland in north eastern India has long been known for its tribal way of life. The Naga people, with their tattooed faces, striking traditional attire and legends of headhunting have always been a source of awe and fearful admiration for travellers to the region.

Heritage

Nagaland has 16 major tribes with several sub-tribes each. Though all of them have warrior lineages, none has a reputation for ferocity like the Konyaks of Mon district. The most feared even among their tribal brethren, they are known to have continued their headhunting ways for many years even after all the others had discontinued the practice.

The Konyaks follow a patriarchal system of society. The angh is the chief of the tribe and his jurisdiction extends over many villages. Though they are men of modest means, their authority over these villages is absolute and they have the final say in all matters big and small, even those involving the local government.

Homestays

The anghs are usually polygamous and one of their wives is the daughter of an angh herself. This qualifies her to be the anghya, or the chief wife. Anghship is hereditary and only a son born to an anghya can succeed his father. Daughters of anghs can only marry sons of anghs and face lifelong banishment if they break this rule. Interestingly, these tribal laws are not a thing of the past and are very much in practice even today.

Homestays

Ask them about their tattoos and you will learn that they are more like war medals than an adornment of choice. In days of yore, a battle returned warrior was awarded a v shaped tattoo on his chest. Face tattoos were privileges reserved for those returning from battle with trophies of enemy heads. The more heads they collected, the more intricate the tattoos became, awarding them greater status and esteem in society. These tattoos were etched by the anghya herself, using large thorns from the surrounding jungles. The process was painful and sometimes faces stayed swollen for days afterwards but it was an honour that the warriors would not give up for anything.

The skulls brought back from battle were displayed in houses as symbols of bravery but of late there has been a movement to bury these and do away with the memories of what is today regarded by many community members as a cruel tradition.

The Konyaks are natural sculptors. They carve wood, metal and animal bones to make fabulous masks and figures, cane baskets, bead and metal jewellery.
