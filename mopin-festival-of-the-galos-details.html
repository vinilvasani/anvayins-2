ARUNACHAL PRADESH

Mopin Festival of the Galos

The Galo tribe of Arunachal Pradesh is a primarily agrarian society. Consequently its most important festival, the Mopin, falls during the sowing season. The tribespeople pray to Mopin Ane, the Goddess of prosperity, for a bounteous harvest and the banishment of evil spirits.

Unlike other Arunachali festivals which originated as family affairs, Mopin has always been a community festival. Though it is celebrated in all major towns and villages across East and West Siang districts, the biggest celebration takes place at Aalo, headquarter of the West Siang district.

The festival usually commences on March 31 st with a few days of cultural and sports competitions. It begins to gather steam by April 5 th when residents of Aalo and the surrounding villages start thronging the festival grounds located next to the Donyi-Polo temple.

Galo women rush up to welcome you as you enter the grounds, applying rice paste on your cheeks and offering packets of steamed rice cakes wrapped in a local leaf called ekkam. Accompanying the packets is a bottle of apong, the delicious home-brewed rice beer of the Galos.

Cultural programs are scheduled for each evening and are held in a hall called gumin kinn, meaning ‘prosperity centre’. You will see the participants milling about the grounds in their traditional costumes—the men in white shirts, trousers and shoes with jackets embroidered in black in typical Galo designs called tango; the women in white tops and white wraparounds called gale and colourful traditional bead jewellery. The performances include the dance enactment of a typical Galo wedding and a local woman singing a lullaby to her child, in addition to folk and contemporary songs and dances.

April 6 is the most important day of the festival. A group of about 400 women dressed in traditional costume perform a synchronised dance called the mega popir. This is followed by the beheading of the sacrificial bison called mithun which has been awaiting its fate tied to a sacred bamboo pole in the middle of the festival grounds. According to local belief, the blood of the sacrificed mithun is believed to bring good luck and you will see Galo women collecting drops of it to take back home. The act of sacrifice is an important local tradition and men and women crowd around to witness it, thereby also saving animal lovers or those with weaker stomachs the trouble of having to watch the spectacle.

Though the festival is officially over after the mithun sacrifice, we highly recommend an extra day’s stay so you can be a part of the procession that makes its way across villages around Aalo. Apart from a chance to sample the delicious local food and apong served to the participants at every household, it is also a splendid opportunity to see the well organised Galo villages and get a peek into the interesting Galo lifestyle.
