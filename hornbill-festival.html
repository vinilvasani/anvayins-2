Nagaland

Hornbill Festival

The Hornbill festival held every year in the Indian state of Nagaland is a ten day long cultural extravaganza. Named after the colourful tropical bird that abounds in this region, the festival was conceived a couple of decades ago to provide an opportunity to the 16 Naga tribes to gather and showcase various aspects of their culture and tradition, thereby also ensuring their survival.

History

The venue at Kisama, on the outskirts of Kohima on the historic Kohima-Imphal highway, is modelled after a traditional Naga village. The variety in performances at the Hornbill festival is unparalleled anywhere in the region. Apart from a medley of melodious folk songs and spirited dances, there are also playacts exhibiting myriad aspects of Naga life present and past–sowing and harvesting, traditional hunting methods, the celebrations around a kill, age old ways of lighting fires without the use of matchsticks, unique methods of fishing and the ensuing squabbles over the catch.

There are also a number of sporting competitions including some very entertaining ones like one-legged fights, mass wrestling among stark naked boys, archery and everyone’s favourite, the tug of war. The atmosphere is not only celebratory but also very warm and welcoming. The performers are most amenable to chatting and posing for pictures with visitors. The entire event is well organised and programs take place on time. Every performance is accompanied by narratives that describe it in lucid detail.

Preparations

Take a walk around the Morungs or traditional Naga houses of learning, each designed according to the tribe’s own unique architecture. On display in these are various forms of Naga art, handicraft and handloom. Feel the thrill of shooting a real rifle or get yourself clicked posing with heavy duty machine guns and AK 47s at the war and artillery museum. The soldiers on duty are courteous and forthcoming with the history of the great wars of the region–the Japanese invasion during World War II and the Chinese invasion of 1962.

And the stage is set

After all the visual treats, you can head to the food stalls for some exotic Naga dishes. Boiled snails and fried silk worms are big crowd pullers but before the vegetarians flee in panic– there’s enough non-grubby food on sale too. Wash it all down with the locally brewed rice beer called chhang served in bamboo mugs. Every stall lends its own special taste to the chhang and you can easily spend a couple of days sampling a mug from each.

The celebrations aren’t limited to Kisama as Kohima comes alive with street-side bands and bustling night markets. While here, do remember to take a walk around the Kohima War Cemetery dedicated to soldiers who laid down their lives in the Second World War. The mood is doubly joyous as the largely Christian population prepares to celebrate Christmas just a few days after Hornbill ends.

Activities

The end of the Hornbill festival is marked with a huge bonfire in the middle of the festival ground. Spectators are invited to join in as all the performers come out to dance together by the fire in a grand culmination to a magical ten days.
