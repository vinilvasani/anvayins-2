Assam

Forest Man of India

India has had its share of passionate environmentalists but few can match the single-minded focus and perseverance of Jadav Molai Payeng. Born in Assam in 1963 into a poor family with 13 siblings, a young Molai was distraught when he returned to his village after his school examinations–dead snakes were everywhere and cattle refused to come out of the ponds that gave them respite from the intense heat caused by an absence of any tree cover. Jadav consulted the village elders and was advised to plant bamboo trees. He took to the task in all earnest.

When the forest department launched an afforestation drive in the area, Jadav joined that party as a daily wage labourer and did not stop planting more trees even after the formal initiative had ended. After 30 years of the same work, Jadav transformed the once barren river island into a lush forest that is home to tigers, rhinoceros, elephants, wild buffaloes, deer and numerous varieties of birds. He worked quietly in his beloved forest for many years before a journalist from Assam published a piece on him and made him known to the world. Felicitations and awards followed.

In 2012, Jadav Payeng was honoured as the Forest Man of India by the Jawaharlal University and later that year, then President APJ Abdul Kalam felicitated him with a cash prize. Also in that year, he was invited to speak at the International Forum for Sustainable Development at Evian in France. The forest that Jadav planted was named Molai Forest after him and in 2015 he was awarded the 4 th highest civilian prize of India, the Padma Shri.

Even today, Jadav will wake up at 3 am to go and work in his forest. This also keeps poachers at bay since they usually operate in the wee hours of the morning. He’s incredibly happy that the government has allowed him to afforest another 500 hectares.

Meeting Jadav Payeng teaches one a lesson or two in humility. Try complimenting him for the great work he’s done and he’ll be quick to give credit to the forest ecosystem. He believes that he only nudged nature along by sowing a few seeds–the trees grew and bore fruit, the birds ate the fruit and scattered the seeds from which more trees grew and so the process continued. Jadav prefers not to be associated with NGOs or and refuses any more felicitations but uses every opportunity to encourage children to plant trees. He thinks that the world will be a much greener place if every student plants at least two trees and nurtures it for five years after that.

A day spent in the Molai Forest is truly rewarding. A short trek along a river bank followed by a boat ride across another stream and you’re there. As you walk through a small village, you might come across bullock carts carrying elephant grass or hay. If empty, the driver will be only too happy to give you a ride to Jadav Payeng’s hut. Always in a simple vest and loin cloth, Jadav talks passionately about his life’s work. A man with modest educational qualification, this Gandhian has proved that you can be the change you wish to see in the world.
