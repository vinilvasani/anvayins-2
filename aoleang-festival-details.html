Aoleang Festival

Aoleang is the most important annual festival of the Konyak Naga tribe of Nagaland in northeastern India. The largest in the region, the Konyaks are primarily an agrarian tribe and practice slash and burn cultivation, also known as jhum in the local tongue. Aoleang is celebrated at the end of the sowing season to usher in spring with its blooming red coral trees and red lilies signaling the beginning of a new year. The Konyaks use the opportunity to pray to the divine spirits for a good harvest.

The festival is celebrated in April from the first to the sixth. The first three days are called Hoi Lah Nyih, Yin Mok Pho Nyih and Mok Shek Nyih and are spent preparing food and rice beer, weaving colourful traditional cloth and collecting animals for sacrifice during the festival.

The most important day of the festival varies from village to village; at Mon, the district headquarters, it is the fourth day called Lingnyu Nyih. The people dress up in their colourful best, accessorized with beautiful traditional jewellery. They spend the entire day dancing, singing, feasting and merrymaking. Men, women, boys and girls break into impromptu traditional dances and songs whenever the mood strikes. Groups of men and women from each khel, or colony inside the village, make their way to the village chief’s house to pay their respects. They are led by the deputy chief’s wife, and traditional dances are performed by the princesses of various villages that fall under the chief’s jurisdiction.

Things become truly exciting at the arrival of groups of men in traditional costume with faces covered in war paint and old muzzle loading guns and machetes slung over their shoulders. They charge at each other in mock headhunting battles, letting out shrill war cries, waving their machetes and shooting their guns. The atmosphere is electric as the air fills with gun smoke and battle cries. Among the men you will spot some tattooed warriors of days gone by, glad for an opportunity to relive their headhunting days.

The final two days of the festival are called Lingha Nyih and Lingshan Nyih. This time is dedicated to family and is spent in cleaning the house as well as the village.

Overall, the Aoleang celebrations are a riot of coIour and activity. This festival is also more traditional than many others in northeastern India. Visit the Mon district in the month of April and witness the Aoleang festival in its full glory.
