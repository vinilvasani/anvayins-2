The Galos of Aalo

The Galos of Arunachal Pradesh live predominantly in the middle eastern part of the state and are among the more populous tribes of the region. There are two major district headquarters in this part of Arunachal Pradesh, Aalo and Besar, which though infamous for their poor road connectivity are great starting points for exploring the surrounding Galo villages.

Heritage

Most Galo villages are clean and well-planned. Walk around one and you will find neatly arrayed rows of thatched houses raised on stilts on either side of the main street. The space beneath these houses was used traditionally for storing firewood and housing livestock but over time the animals were moved to separate shelters in the backyard, to maintain better hygiene around the house. You will also see locally engineered granaries which are very effective at keeping rodents away. Stroll by the community hall and sports grounds and watch a game of volleyball or football in progress which the players will be more than happy to have you join.

The Galos have a strong sense of family and community and all their customs and practices reflect this. The first syllable of any child’s name is the last syllable of the father’s name. Children, women and the elderly are valued and honoured—child marriage is strictly prohibited, women have absolute rights over family jewellery and get a good share of the family property and taking care of ageing parents is not only encouraged but enforced through laws that limit the inheritance rights of those who do not.

Festivals

The Galos also are probably among the most disciplined amongst all the Arunachali tribes and their daily life is governed by a set of laws called bogum boka, conceptualised and drafted by the village elders. These laws are fair to everyone irrespective of age, gender or class and in some ways are rather progressive. For example, live-in relationships are not only condoned but the partners can also expect moral support from the village council if the relationship is opposed by the concerned families. The punishment for infidelity is the same for women as it is for men and is usually suffered in terms of excommunication from the community or fines of cows or mithun. Hear firsthand accounts of all this and more as you chat with members of the Galo tribe on lunch or teatime visits to their houses.

Homestays

The Galos are primarily an agrarian society and practise jhum cultivation. This involves clearing specific land areas of all vegetation through controlled fires, farming them for two or three years and then leaving them fallow for the next 10 to 12 years during which time the soil replenishes its nutrient content. Jhum has often been criticised for being ecologically damaging but the locals feel otherwise. They point out that it is practised only on private land approved by the village council and not in forest areas. Moreover, large trees and medicinal plants are left untouched. Members of the household owning the land are required to keep a strict vigil during the burning phase to prevent the fire from spreading.

In fact, the locals believe that the process is ecologically superior to other agricultural methods because the carbon from the ash neutralises the soil acidity thereby obliterating the need for fertilisers and other chemicals. By timing the activities of sowing and harvesting according to the lunar calendar—apparently pests are most active during the full moon period and least active during new moon—they are able to completely avoid pesticides as well. Witness whole villages coming together to clear a new piece of land or to farm a ready field.

The Galos are a warm, hospitable and unique people and a visit to their native land will give you an authentic and eye-opening experience of life in the far north east of India.

Experiences

Yet another holy place is the Samten Yongcha monastery, popularly known as the Old Gompa, and believed to be older than the more famous Tawang Monastery. While it is believed to be over 400 years old, the present structure was rebuilt about 100 years ago after the original building burnt down in a massive fire. Among the idols at the monastery, the local favourite is Tardin Chejlingb, tales of whose spiritual powers are told reverently. The locals believe that during the time of the great fire, the idol of Tardin Chejlingb made its way out of the gompa on its own and was thus unscathed. It is the one to look out for when visiting the monastery.
